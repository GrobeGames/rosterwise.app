## Why win-loss records are a starting point, not an answer

It's natural to look at a team's record first. A 15-3-2 season looks better than a 6-12 season. But records without context can be actively misleading.

A team that went 6-12 in a conference with seven nationally ranked opponents may have been more competitive than a team that went 15-3-2 against a weaker schedule. A program that had a down year because of a coaching transition might be poised for a significant upswing. A team with a strong record built on seniors is about to look very different next fall.

Records reflect one season's results. They don't tell you about roster stability, coaching philosophy, player development, academic culture, or the trajectory a program is on. Those are the factors that determine whether a program is a good fit for your athlete over four (or five) years, not just next season.

## Coaching tenure and stability

The head coach is the single most important factor in a program's identity. Coaching tenure tells you several things:

**Long-tenured coaches (8+ years)** generally indicate program stability, an established recruiting philosophy, and institutional support. Players committed under a long-tenured coach are more likely to experience continuity in playing style, team culture, and the relationships that influenced their commitment.

**New coaches (1-3 years)** may be rebuilding. This isn't inherently bad — a new coach might bring energy, new connections, and a fresh approach. But a coaching change resets many of the assumptions a family made during the recruiting process. Players who committed to the previous coach sometimes find that the new coach has different plans for their position or playing style.

**Frequent coaching turnover** (multiple coaches in a short span) is a signal worth investigating. It could indicate institutional instability, insufficient investment in the sport, or other issues.

**What to ask about coaching:**
- How long has the head coach been at the program?
- What is the coaching staff's typical tenure? Do assistants stay, or is there high turnover?
- Has the coaching staff changed since your athlete began communicating with the program?
- What is the coach's stated recruiting philosophy — development-focused or win-now?

Coaching tenure data is publicly available and is one of the most underused pieces of information in the recruiting process. RosterWise tracks head coach tenure for every program we cover, because we believe it's that important.

## Roster composition and stability

A roster tells you far more than a record does. Here's what to look for:

**Class-year distribution.** A roster heavy with seniors is about to have significant turnover — which could mean more playing time for incoming freshmen. A roster with few seniors and many sophomores might be deep but young, meaning an incoming freshman faces intense competition from players who have already developed in the system.

**Position depth.** How many players share your athlete's position? If a program has six center backs and your athlete is a center back, the path to playing time is different than at a program with two. This is straightforward but often overlooked.

**International composition.** Some programs recruit heavily from international pipelines. This affects roster composition, playing style, and the number of spots available to domestic recruits. Neither heavy nor light international recruiting is inherently good or bad, but families should understand the pattern at any program they're considering.

**Roster size relative to the norm.** Under the House settlement, D1 programs that opted in have a 28-player roster limit. Programs that haven't opted in may carry larger rosters. Roster size affects competition for spots, team dynamics, and walk-on opportunities.

**Transfer activity.** A roster with many transfers in could indicate a coach who actively recruits the transfer portal. A roster with many transfers out could indicate players who weren't satisfied — or simply a normal level of turnover. Context matters.

## Conference strength and competitive context

The conference a program plays in shapes the daily competitive experience more than most families realize.

**Within D1 alone, the range of conference strength is enormous.** The Atlantic Coast Conference and the Big Ten have historically been among the strongest in men's and women's soccer. But strong programs exist in every conference, and a player who thrives in one competitive environment might struggle in another — or vice versa.

**Questions to ask about conference fit:**
- What is the conference's typical level of play relative to other conferences in the same division?
- How does the program typically finish within its conference?
- What does the non-conference schedule look like? (Programs that play tough non-conference schedules are testing themselves against broader competition.)
- Does the conference's style of play align with your athlete's strengths?

**Don't overvalue conference prestige.** A player who starts and plays significant minutes at a program in a mid-major conference is likely developing faster than a player who sits on the bench at a program in a power conference. Playing time is the most important development tool in college soccer.

## Academic fit

For families who are approaching the recruiting process thoughtfully, academic fit should be weighted at least as heavily as athletic fit. Consider:

**Graduation rates.** The NCAA publishes Academic Success Rates (ASR) and Federal Graduation Rates for D1 and D2 programs. These numbers tell you how many student-athletes who entered the program actually finished with a degree. Significant gaps between the program's graduation rate and the institution's overall rate are worth asking about.

**Academic profile of the institution.** Does the school offer the major your athlete is interested in? What is the academic culture like? Is the school known for particular strengths that align with your family's goals?

**Academic support for athletes.** Most college programs provide some level of academic support — tutoring, study hall requirements, priority registration. The quality and depth of this support varies significantly.

**Class scheduling and travel.** How does the team's competition and travel schedule interact with academic commitments? Programs that travel frequently for away games create different academic challenges than programs with primarily regional schedules.

**The honest reality:** Athletic programs and academic programs serve different masters. A school that's a great athletic fit might not offer your athlete's intended major. A school with the perfect academic program might not have a soccer program that fits. Families who identify the non-negotiables on both sides early in the process save themselves significant time and heartache.

## Facilities and investment

Facilities are visible and easy to compare, which makes them disproportionately influential during campus visits. A few things to keep in perspective:

**Facilities reflect institutional investment in the sport.** A program with a dedicated soccer stadium, quality practice fields, a modern training room, and a team lounge is one where the institution has committed resources to soccer. This matters for player development, injury recovery, and daily quality of life.

**But facilities are not destiny.** Some of the most successful college soccer programs in the country play on fields that don't look like what you see on ESPN. Development happens on the training ground and in the team culture, not in the locker room's square footage.

**What to look at beyond the stadium:**
- Practice field quality and availability (shared fields with other sports can limit training time)
- Training room and sports medicine access
- Strength and conditioning resources (shared with all athletes or soccer-specific?)
- Travel arrangements (bus vs. flights for away games, hotel quality)
- Nutritional support

## Culture and team environment

This is the hardest factor to evaluate and arguably the most important. A few approaches:

**Talk to current players — without the coach present.** Ask about team culture, daily life, the balance between athletics and academics, and what surprised them about the program. Listen for what they don't say as much as what they do.

**Observe a practice if possible.** The dynamic between coaches and players, the intensity of training, and the general atmosphere tell you things that a polished recruiting visit won't.

**Ask about player retention.** How many players from the last two recruiting classes are still on the roster? Players leave programs for many reasons, and some attrition is normal. But a pattern of significant departures is worth understanding.

**Ask about playing time philosophy.** Does the coach rotate frequently? Are freshmen expected to contribute immediately, or is there typically a development year? Is there a clear pathway from practice performance to game minutes?

Every family's priorities are different, and every athlete's personality fits different environments. A high-intensity, results-driven culture is perfect for some players and wrong for others. There is no objectively "best" culture — only the right fit for your athlete.

## Financial picture

The full financial picture includes more than the athletic scholarship offer:

- **Athletic scholarship amount** (full, partial, or none)
- **Academic merit aid** (many institutions offer significant academic scholarships that can be combined with athletic aid)
- **Need-based financial aid** (determined by FAFSA and the institution's aid policies)
- **Cost of attendance beyond tuition** (housing, meals, books, travel, personal expenses)
- **The four-year trajectory.** Is the scholarship guaranteed for four years, or renewed annually? What conditions affect renewal? What happens if the player is injured?

At D3 programs, where no athletic scholarships exist, the financial conversation is entirely about academic merit aid, need-based aid, and institutional grants. D3 financial aid packages can be substantial — sometimes exceeding what a partial D2 athletic scholarship would provide.

**The scholarship conversation should happen early and honestly.** Families who wait until late in the recruiting process to discuss finances sometimes discover that the program they've fallen in love with is financially out of reach. Ask direct questions about the full cost of attendance and realistic financial aid expectations before investing significant emotional energy in a specific program.

## Location and lifestyle

These factors feel less important during the excitement of recruiting but become significant once your athlete is living in that location for four years:

- **Distance from home.** How often can your family realistically visit? How easy is travel?
- **Climate.** A player from southern California may find adjustment to a program in Minnesota challenging — and vice versa.
- **Campus setting.** Urban, suburban, or rural? Each offers a different daily experience.
- **Social environment.** What is campus life like outside of soccer? Does the broader student experience align with your athlete's personality and interests?

## Putting it all together

No program will score perfectly on every factor. The evaluation process is about understanding trade-offs and identifying which factors are non-negotiable for your family.

A practical approach:

1. **Identify your non-negotiables** (academic program, geographic range, division level, minimum financial aid threshold)
2. **Filter broadly** to create a realistic list of potential programs
3. **Evaluate each program** across the factors above — not just the visible ones like record and facilities, but the structural ones like coaching tenure, roster composition, and graduation rates
4. **Visit with specific questions prepared.** A campus visit without a framework is tourism; a campus visit with a framework is research.
5. **Revisit your list regularly.** Programs change. Coaches leave. Your athlete develops. The list that made sense in sophomore year may need updating by senior year.

The families who navigate this process best are the ones who evaluate programs honestly — including the programs that are recruiting their athlete hardest. Flattery feels good. Fit is what matters.

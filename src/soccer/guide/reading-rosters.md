## Why rosters matter more than rankings

Rankings tell you which teams are winning. Rosters tell you which teams need players.

A recruiting family's job isn't to find the best team — it's to find the best fit. And fit is determined by the composition of a specific program's roster in the year your athlete arrives. A nationally ranked team with eight defenders and your athlete plays defense is a different proposition than an unranked team with two defenders at the same position who are both seniors.

Every college soccer program publishes its roster on the school's athletics website. This is public information, freely accessible, and deeply informative — if you know what to look for.

## What a roster page typically shows

Most college soccer roster pages include some combination of:

- **Player name**
- **Jersey number**
- **Position** (sometimes detailed, sometimes just "MF" or "D")
- **Class year** (Fr., So., Jr., Sr., sometimes with "R-" prefix for redshirt or "Gr." for graduate students)
- **Height and weight** (more common in men's programs)
- **Hometown and previous school** (high school, club team, or previous college)
- **Country of origin** (sometimes listed explicitly, sometimes inferred from hometown)

The specific format varies by website platform. Some programs publish detailed rosters with photos and bios; others list basic information in a table. The data is there — it just takes attention to read it.

## Reading class-year distribution

This is the single most actionable piece of roster intelligence for a recruiting family.

**Count the players at each class year.** If a roster has eight seniors, seven juniors, six sophomores, and four freshmen, that program is about to lose eight players to graduation. Those eight spots need to be filled through recruiting and transfers. If your athlete plays a position held by several of those departing seniors, the opportunity is real.

Conversely, a roster with twelve freshmen and eleven sophomores may be stacked with young talent. An incoming freshman faces heavy competition from players who have already been developing in the system.

**Look for class-year gaps at specific positions.** A program might have a balanced overall roster but a significant gap at one position. If there are three senior midfielders, one sophomore midfielder, and no junior or freshman midfielders, that program has a clear positional need emerging.

**Redshirt and graduate students complicate the picture.** A "senior" who is actually a redshirt junior has another year of eligibility. A graduate student who transferred in is taking a roster spot but will be gone after one year. Understanding who is actually leaving and who might return requires reading beyond the basic class-year label.

## Reading position depth

Position depth is straightforward in concept but often overlooked in practice.

**Map every player to a position group.** Goalkeepers, defenders, midfielders, and forwards — or more detailed groupings if the roster provides them (center back, outside back, defensive midfielder, etc.). Count how many players are in each group.

**Compare position depth to typical lineup needs.** A soccer team starts 11 players. A typical formation might include 1 goalkeeper, 4 defenders, 3-4 midfielders, and 2-3 forwards. If a program has seven midfielders and three forwards, midfield is deep and the forward group might be looking for players.

**Cross-reference with class year.** Position depth alone isn't enough. A position group with five players is deep — unless three of them are seniors. In that case, the position will have two returning players next year and a clear need.

**Account for positional flexibility.** Many college soccer players can play multiple positions. A player listed as a midfielder might also play outside back. This means position depth charts based solely on listed positions are approximate, not exact. It's still the best publicly available indicator of where needs exist.

## Reading international composition

International players are a significant part of college soccer rosters, particularly in men's D1 and D2 programs. The patterns differ meaningfully between men's and women's soccer.

**What to look for:**
- How many players on the roster are from outside the United States?
- What countries or regions are represented?
- Are international players concentrated at specific positions?
- How has the international composition changed over the last few seasons (if you can access previous rosters)?

**Why it matters for domestic recruits:** A roster with a high percentage of international players has fewer spots available for domestic recruits. This isn't a judgment — international recruiting reflects coaching philosophy, connections, and program identity. But a family evaluating fit needs to understand the competitive landscape accurately.

**Why it matters for international recruits:** Programs with a history of international recruiting are more likely to have the infrastructure (visa support, cultural adjustment resources) to support international student-athletes.

## Reading hometown patterns

Where players come from tells you about a program's recruiting geography and pipeline.

**Regional concentration.** A program where most players come from within a 200-mile radius recruits differently than one that draws from across the country. Regional programs may be more open to local recruits they can evaluate in person.

**Out-of-state patterns.** If a program in the Southeast consistently has players from the Northeast, that suggests a deliberate recruiting pipeline in that region — and possibly a connection to specific clubs or coaches there.

**Previous school information.** When a roster lists the player's previous school — whether that's a high school, club team, or previous college — it reveals the pathways that feed into the program. If you see several players from the same club or academy, that club has a relationship with the coaching staff.

**Transfer sources.** Players listed as transfers from other colleges tell you about the program's approach to the transfer portal. A program with many transfers in is actively recruiting experienced players, which changes the competitive dynamic for incoming freshmen.

## Reading height and size profiles

Height and weight data (when available) provides context for physical fit:

**Position-specific norms.** Goalkeepers tend to be taller. Center backs are often taller than outside backs. Forwards show more size variation than any other position group. Comparing your athlete's size to the current roster at their position gives you a realistic sense of physical fit.

**Program-specific tendencies.** Some coaches prefer bigger, more physical players. Others prioritize speed and technical ability regardless of size. The current roster's size profile reflects the coach's preference, whether stated or not.

**Don't overweight this factor.** Size is one dimension of fit. A technically gifted 5'6" midfielder can thrive at a program where the average midfielder is 5'10" if the coach values what that player brings. Size profiles are context, not a filter.

## What rosters don't tell you

Roster analysis has real limitations. Honest assessment of what you can't see is important:

**Playing time.** A roster tells you who's on the team, not who plays. A player listed as a sophomore forward might have started every game or might not have seen the field.

**Injury status.** Players on the roster might be injured and unable to compete. This affects depth and opportunity, but it's not visible from roster data alone.

**Incoming recruiting class.** The current roster doesn't show who has already committed for next year. A position that looks thin might already have two incoming freshmen committed. This information usually becomes public through recruiting announcements and signing-day press releases, but the timing varies.

**Coaching plans.** A coach might be planning to shift a player from midfield to defense, changing the position depth picture entirely. Roster analysis gives you the starting point for a conversation — not the final answer.

**Transfer portal activity.** Players might be entering or exiting the transfer portal. This is a dynamic process that roster snapshots don't capture.

## A practical approach for families

Here's a process for using roster analysis in your recruiting strategy:

1. **Start with your athlete's position and class year.** These two factors determine the most relevant roster questions.

2. **Pull up the roster for each target program.** Count players at your athlete's position. Note their class years. Identify who's likely graduating.

3. **Look for class-year gaps.** Programs with thin classes at your athlete's position in the year they'd enter are worth prioritizing.

4. **Note the international composition.** Understand the competitive landscape for domestic spots.

5. **Check hometown patterns.** If a program recruits heavily from your athlete's region, there may be existing connections to leverage.

6. **Compare across programs.** The same athlete might face heavy competition at one program and fill an obvious need at another. Both programs might be equally good fits academically and culturally — but the roster composition tells you where the opportunity is greater.

7. **Update regularly.** Rosters change. Players transfer. New commitments are announced. The analysis you did in September may need refreshing in January.
